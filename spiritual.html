Becoming Through Christlike Discipleship

Life is not just about what I accomplish, but about who I am becoming. Elder Dallin H. Oaks taught that the gospel is designed to shape who we become, not merely to govern what we do. In my cybersecurity journey, technical skills are important, but qualities like patience, humility, and diligence are essential for a reliable professional and leader. Maintaining composure and showing respect for others is as essential as technical knowledge in creating a vibrant workplace. As I advance in this course and my career, I’m excited to intentionally cultivate Christlike qualities in my project management, conflict resolution, and team leadership, enriching our collaborative journey.

Honoring Sacred Covenants in All Aspects of Life

The principle that the Savior's Atonement isn't just for repentance, but also a source of daily strength, healing, and enabling power, has been a crucial concept in my life. Whether I am at school, work, or just in my personal life, each comes with its own set of challenges. There have been days when I feel stretched a little too thin or unsure if I can keep up with expectations. It's moments like these that I rely on Christ's Atonement to help give me the resilience, clarity, and peace I need. Professionally, this reminds me that I don't need to be perfect or have all the answers. Because mistakes and setbacks are inevitable, I can continue to move forward, learn, and improve thanks to the Atonement of Christ. This principle has helped guide me in striving to be spiritually grounded, even in demanding work environments.

Becoming Through Christlike Discipleship

My temple covenants are a foundational part of who I am. They remind me of my eternal identity and the responsibilities that come with it. In every setting — whether church, home, or workplace — I want those sacred commitments to shape my choices. In cybersecurity trust and integrity are non-negotiable. Being able to handle sensitive information and secure a network environment requires the use of integrity, including honesty, discretion, and a commitment to protect others. The covenants I make help me maintain a higher standard in these areas of responsibility. This principle helps me to remain accountable not only to employers and clients but also to the Lord, ensuring that my career and discipleship stay in harmony.

Ether 12:27 — Strength Through Weakness

This scripture has anchored me during moments of technical struggle—especially when something in the lab wasn’t working, or I hit a wall diagnosing a system issue. In my home lab experiments and internship trials, I’ve found that growth often came not from success, but from wrestling with failure. Ether 12:27 reminds me that weakness isn’t disqualifying—it’s refining. It encourages me to approach problems with humility and patience, knowing Christ can transform weakness into strength when I’m willing to keep trying and leaning on Him.

×

Way to Be – Gordon B. Hinckley

Hinckley's book became a compass early in my journey. The simplicity and clarity of his "nine Be’s" stood out: Be Humble, Be Prayerful, Be Positive. As I built out my professional skills, like conducting risk assessments and implementing STIG compliance, it struck me that technical excellence means little without a moral compass. Each “Be” encourages me to navigate the cybersecurity world with a kind of ethical intentionality, like keeping user privacy at the forefront or advocating honesty in reporting vulnerabilities.

Temple Worker Experience

Becoming a temple worker wasn’t just a title, it was a transformational responsibility. There were quiet lessons in reverence, stewardship, and consistency. This calling showed me the value of sacred duty, and I’ve carried that sense of stewardship into how I handle sensitive systems and data. When I was building out Active Directory environments or establishing VPNs, I wasn’t just doing technical work, I was honoring a covenant of trust.

×

"Behold Thy Mother" – Jeffrey R. Holland

One part of this talk that truly struck me was Elder Holland's gentle reminder: “You are doing better than you think you are.” That single phrase pierced through the noise of self-doubt I’ve felt in school, work, and life. It reminded me that progress isn't always visible, and that my efforts—even when imperfect—are noticed by heaven. In my cybersecurity coursework and hands-on labs, there are times I wonder if I’m keeping up, doing enough, or reaching the level I aspire to. Elder Holland’s words reassured me that the Lord sees the small victories I often overlook.

This talk has increased my compassion toward others, as well as an understanding that everyone is doing their best in ways we may never know. When it comes to supporting those around me, whether it be in troubleshooting or writing documentation for less technical users, I want to be the kind of person who sees potential and encourages growth.

This verse has been a personal lifeline for me. When deadlines loom, troubleshooting becomes overwhelming, or life throws curveballs, I return to this. It’s helped me reset when I’ve struggled to balance faith, school, and work.

Gratitude in Daily Challenges

Even when I face frustrating technical problems or tight deadlines, I try to pause and recognize the small blessings around me—like supportive colleagues, learning opportunities, or even the simple gift of patience. This mindset of gratitude brings peace and reinforces my spiritual resilience.

In the scriptures, we are reminded to “give thanks in all things” (1 Thessalonians 5:18). This counsel encourages me to cultivate a spirit of thankfulness not only in joyous moments but also during trials. When I acknowledge God’s hand in the details of my life, I gain strength to persevere with hope rather than frustration.

Practicing gratitude daily helps me to reframe challenges as opportunities for growth. It nurtures humility, reminding me that every success is a gift, and every setback is a lesson. This perspective deepens my faith and prepares me to serve others with a cheerful heart.

×

Teaching as a Spiritual Practice

Teaching others is more than sharing knowledge—it’s a spiritual discipline that helps me grow in empathy, patience, and humility. As I mentor or help classmates, I am reminded that everyone is on a unique journey and that learning happens at different paces.

The Savior taught through parables and personal example, showing that teaching is a form of service. When I engage in teaching, I follow His example by seeking to uplift and inspire rather than simply inform.

This practice fosters my spiritual growth because it requires me to listen deeply, to understand struggles, and to be patient. It is through serving others in this way that I feel my faith strengthened and my love for my fellow learners increased.

One of my favorite quotes is from Doctrine and Covenants 88:122, which urges us to “seek learning, even by study and also by faith.” Teaching invites me to embody that principle daily, by sharing what I have learned and trusting in the Spirit to guide both myself and others.

×

Hope Amid Uncertainty

Life and work often present situations full of uncertainty—whether it’s a new project with unknown outcomes or changes in my career path. Trusting that God’s plan is unfolding, even when I can’t see the full picture, gives me peace and courage.

The scriptures teach that hope is “an anchor to the souls of men” (Ether 12:4). This hope is not mere optimism, but a deep assurance rooted in faith that God’s promises are true and that He is actively involved in our lives.

When I face ambiguity or setbacks, I remind myself that patience and endurance are spiritual gifts that strengthen character and bring me closer to divine purposes. Holding onto hope helps me maintain focus and resilience.

In moments of doubt, prayer and scripture study renew my hope, reminding me that even when the path is unclear, I am never alone. This spiritual hope fuels my determination to keep moving forward with trust and humility.
